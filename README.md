# econ5200-replication-study

# ECON 5200 — Replication Study and Extension, Emily Perras

**Card, D., & Krueger, A. B. (1994)**  
*Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry in New Jersey and Pennsylvania.*

This project replicates one of the most influential empirical papers in labor economics, which challenged the traditional prediction that minimum wage increases reduce employment.

## Issue and Findings

The main finding is that increasing the minimum wage did not lead to job losses. In New Jersey, employment rose slightly from about 20.4 to 21.0 workers per restaurant, while in Pennsylvania it fell from about 23.3 to 21.2. Overall, this results in a net increase of about 2.75 workers per restaurant after the policy change.

We also find no evidence that restaurants raised prices to offset higher wages. Across soda, fries, and entrée items, price changes were essentially zero, suggesting little to no price pass-through.

---

## Methodology

This analysis compares two similar groups: restaurants in New Jersey (which raised wages) and restaurants in Pennsylvania (which did not).

You can think of this like tracking two similar businesses over time. If both are moving in similar ways and then one changes a policy, any difference that appears afterward can be attributed to that change. This approach allows us to approximate a real experiment using real-world data.

---

## Visual Analysis

The main graph shows estimated price changes across different fast food chains after the wage increase. Each point represents the estimated effect, and the horizontal lines show the range of uncertainty.

The key takeaway is that almost all estimates are very close to zero. For example, the overall estimated change in soda prices is about -0.0001, and even after adding additional controls, it remains near zero at +0.0004. Most chains show no meaningful change, while one chain (Wendy’s) shows a small decrease of about -0.05.

Overall, the visual clearly shows there is no consistent evidence of price increases following the policy.

---

## What This Means

These results suggest that moderate increases in the minimum wage may not reduce employment or lead to higher prices, contrary to common assumptions.

For policymakers, this means wage increases can improve worker income without necessarily harming jobs. For businesses, it suggests that firms may adjust in other ways rather than cutting labor or raising prices.

Overall, this project highlights the importance of relying on data when evaluating economic policy, since real-world outcomes can differ from what standard theory might predict.
